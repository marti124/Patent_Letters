Sheriff Mailing Package Generator

Version 22

Generate personalized cover letters, county savings reports, and brochures for any U.S. county sheriff. Works for one county or hundreds.

Covers counties across 46 states with elected county sheriffs. Connecticut, Alaska, Hawaii, and Rhode Island are excluded because they do not have sheriffs or sheriff-equivalent positions with standing to bring a constitutional court challenge.

Background: Why This Tool Was Created

The article “Should a Sheriff Sue Over the $22T Taking? The Standing Gamble and Publicity Strategy” — accessible at x.com/RoleighMartin (X Articles) or Substack — presents a constitutional argument that has never been raised in court.

The U.S. Constitution’s Patent Clause (Article I, Section 8, Clause 8) grants patent protection for “limited Times” — meaning that after a patent expires, the design enters the public domain for the public to use freely. But energy and safety regulations enacted after 1970 make it impossible to remanufacture any vehicle or machine whose patents have expired. The designs never enter a functional public domain. The original patent holders never face competition from remanufactured alternatives. The “limited Times” protection becomes, de facto, infinite — a permanent monopoly the Founders explicitly prohibited.

The economic consequence is staggering. American households overpay an estimated $2,420 per year on vehicles, equipment, and appliances because the competition the Patent Clause promises has been regulatory-blocked for over fifty years. Counties overpay for every patrol vehicle, fire truck, and piece of road equipment they purchase.

Any of the nation’s 3,081 elected county sheriffs has standing to challenge this in federal court. Each sheriff’s county is directly harmed — paying inflated prices for fleet vehicles and equipment in a market where remanufacturing options are blocked and manufacturers face no competitive pressure to discount. Public interest law firms such as Pacific Legal Foundation provide free legal representation, so the challenge costs the county nothing.

This tool was created so that any reader who wants to see the Patent Clause promise restored — and their cost of living reduced — can communicate directly with a sheriff about this issue. The goal: get one or more sheriffs to file this constitutional challenge.

What This Tool Generates

From just two inputs — a state and a county — the tool auto-generates a 5-page mailing package:

  1. Cover Letter (1 page) — personalized with your contact info, the sheriff’s name and mailing address, and auto-calculated savings figures
  2. Appendix C County Savings Report (1 page) — a detailed 6-category cost savings breakdown with a professional table
  3. Constitutional Brochure (3 pages) — the full Patent Clause analysis, evidence, precedent, and call to action

Two Ways to Run This Tool

This tool works in any web browser, but it becomes more powerful when run inside a Claude AI interface because Claude can automatically look up sheriff names and addresses via web search.

Option A: Any Web Browser (No Account Required)

Best for: Quick use, anyone with a browser, no sign-up needed.

  1. Open the HTML file by double-clicking it. It opens in your default browser.
  2. Enter your personal information and click “Save My Info for Next Time” to store your details for future sessions.
  3. Click “Generate Sheriff Cover Letters & County Savings Reports” to proceed.
  4. Select a state and county from the dropdowns.
  5. Click “Look Up Sheriff Name & Address” — this opens a Google search in a new browser tab.

Important tips for the Google search tab:

  • Search results typically show the sheriff’s office address more readily than the sheriff’s name. Start by copying the address.
  • The Google search opens in a separate tab. Navigate back to the original tab (where the generator is running) to paste the information.
  • Fill in the address first, then go back to the Google tab to find the sheriff’s name.
  • When copying the sheriff’s name, do not paste the word “Sheriff” — just paste the name itself (e.g., “John Smith” not “Sheriff John Smith”). The cover letter automatically adds the title.

Option B: Inside a Claude AI Interface (AI-Powered Lookup)

Best for: Faster workflow, automatic sheriff name and address lookup.

When run inside Claude, the lookup button calls Claude’s AI with web search to automatically find and fill in the sheriff’s name and office address.

Using Claude.ai directly:

  1. Go to claude.ai and sign in (or sign up).
  2. Start a new conversation and upload the HTML file.
  3. Type “Run” or “Run without displaying a Playground”. Claude renders the tool as an interactive artifact.
  4. When you click the lookup button, Claude searches the web and auto-fills the sheriff fields. Always verify the results: click the “Verify with Google Search” button that appears after lookup — it opens a Google search in a new tab so you can confirm the name and address are correct and current.

Using Claude through Abacus.AI or other platforms:

  1. Upload the HTML file to the chat interface.
  2. Type “Run without displaying a Playground” to render immediately. Without this instruction, some platforms slowly display each HTML line, taking many minutes.
  3. The AI lookup may or may not work depending on the platform. If it fails, it falls back to a Google search tab automatically.

Browser Storage Note

Some browsers (such as Dissenter) block local storage and will not save your information between sessions. Chrome, Edge, Firefox, and Safari all support the “Save My Info” feature. If your browser does not save, you will need to re-enter your information each time.

Batch Mode

Generate packages for multiple counties in one session:

  1. Select a state and county, enter the sheriff’s info.
  2. Click “Add to Batch” to queue it.
  3. Repeat for as many counties as desired (you can mix states).
  4. Click “Preview & Print Entire Batch” — all counties print in sequence, 5 pages each.

Editing the Cover Letter

The cover letter is fully editable in the preview. The toolbar displays “Letter is editable” as a reminder. Click anywhere on the letter text to make changes before printing. The Appendix C and brochure pages are not editable. Edits are not saved between sessions — save as PDF to preserve them.

Saving Your Work

When you click “Save & Print Pages,” the app downloads a self-contained HTML file (Sheriff_Mailing_Package.html) to your Downloads folder. Open that file in your browser and the print dialog will appear automatically. You can then print or select “Save as PDF”:

  • Chrome / Edge / Brave: Select “Save as PDF” from the Destination dropdown.
  • Firefox: Select “Save to PDF” in the print dialog.
  • Safari: Click the PDF dropdown in the lower-left, then “Save as PDF.”

Print settings: Set Margins to “None” and enable “Background graphics” for best results. Paper size: US Letter (8.5” × 11”).

Note: If the print dialog does not appear automatically, press Ctrl+P (Windows) or Cmd+P (Mac) after opening the file.

Excluded States

Four states are excluded because they do not have sheriffs or sheriff-equivalent positions that could have standing in a constitutional court challenge:

  • Connecticut — abolished the office of sheriff in 2000; replaced with state marshals
  • Alaska — the office of sheriff does not exist; law enforcement is handled by state troopers
  • Hawaii — sheriffs are appointed state employees serving at the pleasure of the Director
  • Rhode Island — sheriffs are appointed by the governor

The standing that made Printz v. United States (1997) possible depends on the sheriff holding an independent, elected, constitutionally sovereign county office. Appointed sheriffs lack the political independence to sustain a constitutional challenge.

How the County Savings Report Works

Every number is calculated from a single input: the county’s estimated population (U.S. Census Bureau Vintage 2024). No actual fleet data was obtained. All figures are modeled estimates scaled from a reference county (Boone County, KY, pop. 136,000), validated against Boone County’s actual FY2024 budget data.

Step 1 — Estimate Annual Procurement

The model estimates what the county currently spends annually buying new vehicles and equipment across six categories. Fleet sizes are population-based with adjustment factors; equipment categories use conservative per-capita procurement rates.

Step 2 — Apply Floor and Ceiling Discounts

Floor (15%): Total procurement × 15%. This is the savings if the county keeps buying only new equipment, but remanufactured competition forces prices down — exactly as generic drugs force brand-name drug prices down. The 15% is conservative: the generic drug market shows 30–80% brand-name price drops when generics enter.

Ceiling (45%): Total procurement × 45%. This is the savings if the county purchases remanufactured equipment directly. Based on international pricing: patent-expired vehicles sell for 50–70% less in 90+ countries; 45% is the conservative end of that range.

The Six Categories

Category 1 — Sheriff’s Fleet: Pop. × 0.0006 × adjustment factor (min 12 vehicles). New cost: $55,000 per vehicle. Replacement rate: 19.5%/year. Annual procurement = replacements × $55,000.

Category 2 — Other County Vehicles: Pop. × 0.00032 × adjustment factor (min 6). New cost: $45,000 per vehicle. Replacement rate: 25%/year. Annual procurement = replacements × $45,000.
